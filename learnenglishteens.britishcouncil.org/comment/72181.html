Not feeling well

Gemma and Jack are having a picnic. But Jack doesn't feel well ...

Instructions

Do the preparation exercise first. Then watch the video and follow the instructions to practise your speaking.  

Preparation

Transcript

Gemma: I love picnics. Hey Jack, do you want another sandwich?
Jack: Urgh, no thanks.
Gemma: Oh go on … there’s cheese and tomato or egg.
Jack: No, really, I’m fine.
Gemma: What about some chocolate cake?
Jack: Oh, no thanks, I …
Gemma: What’s wrong?
Jack: I … I … I don’t feel well.
Gemma: Yeah, you don’t look too good.
Jack: Urgh ... I’ve got a headache.
Gemma: You’re a bit red.
Jack: Yeah, can I have some water?
Gemma: Yeah, sure, here you are.
Jack: Thanks.
Gemma: It’s very hot today. Do you want some sun cream?
Jack: No, I’ve got some, but can I wear your hat?
Gemma: Yeah, here you are. Come on, let’s get out of the sun. It’s too hot.
Jack: Yeah, let’s go home.

Submitted by Daiichi on Wed, 10/28/2020 - 03:26

I have a question. In the first dialogue in Gemma's second comment, she says "there’s cheese and tomato or egg." but in the second dialogue, she is saying "there are cheese and tomato or egg." Which is true??
  • Log in or register to post comments

Submitted by JoModerator on Thu, 10/29/2020 - 09:26

Hi Daiichi,
 
Well noticed! Usually is/are agrees with the noun, so 'There are cheese and tomato or egg [sandwiches]' is correct. But in speaking and in some informal writing, we sometimes use 'there’s' even when it refers to more than one. That means 'There's cheese and tomato or egg [sandwiches]' is also correct in informal situations, but it would be incorrect in formal writing or in an exam.
 
If you would like to read more about 'There is', 'there’s' and 'there are', have a look at this page:
https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/there-is-there-s-and-there-are
 
I hope that helps!
 
Best wishes,
Jo (LearnEnglish Teens team)
